Quantum Lab Command Center

Visualizing the invisible — real-time data from one of the world's most advanced quantum computing labs.

data visualization dashboard design enterprise UX quantum computing
Hero screenshot placeholder — Mission Control dashboard

Senior Product Designer
Team
Quantum scientists, engineers, PMs, senior leadership
Timeline
2022 – 2024
Tools
Figma, ReImagine DS, Power BI
The challenge

Microsoft's quantum computing lab generates enormous volumes of real-time data — fridge temperatures, machine states, qubit measurements, experiment results. The scientists and engineers running these experiments needed a way to monitor all of it simultaneously, understand anomalies instantly, and communicate findings to leadership and external partners.

The existing tooling was fragmented and built for engineers, not for the broader team that needed to understand the data. My job was to design a Mission Control room dashboard that could serve multiple audiences at once: highly technical researchers who needed granular data, and executives and visitors who needed to understand the big picture at a glance.

Dashboard wireframe placeholder
User research notes placeholder
The approach

I spent significant time embedded with the quantum team — attending lab sessions, sitting with scientists during experiments, and learning the vocabulary of quantum computing well enough to ask the right design questions. This domain immersion was non-negotiable: you can't visualize data you don't understand.

The key design challenge was hierarchy. A Mission Control display has to work at multiple distances — readable from across a room as a status overview, and readable up close as an operational tool. I established a three-tier hierarchy: primary status indicators (is everything green?), secondary metrics (what are the key readings?), and tertiary detail (drill into any specific measurement).

I also had to design for the unique context of the room itself — large display walls, ambient lighting, mixed audiences from scientists to executives to external visitors from companies like QuNorth who had purchased quantum machines from Microsoft.

Full dashboard layout placeholder
The outcome

The Mission Control dashboard became the primary interface for the quantum lab's operational visibility. It consolidated data from multiple sources into a single coherent view, reduced the time scientists spent context-switching between tools, and gave leadership a real-time window into lab operations that hadn't existed before.

It also became a centrepiece for external visits — when partners and investors toured the facility, the dashboard was the first thing they encountered, setting the tone for conversations about what Microsoft's quantum capabilities actually look like in practice.

Glass Display & AI Assistant

Designing a spatial AI interface for a quantum machine — where visitors become participants.

AI interface hardware-adjacent design interaction design spatial UX
Glass display interface — hero shot placeholder
Role
Senior Product Designer
Team
3D designers, developers, quantum scientists, QuNorth partners
Timeline
2024 (ongoing)
Tools
Figma, Three.js collaboration, Canvas
The challenge

When QuNorth purchased a quantum machine from Microsoft, the physical installation came with a unique design opportunity: a glass display wall fixed directly in front of the machine. This wasn't a screen in the traditional sense — it was a spatial interface, a layer of digital experience sitting between visitors and one of the most advanced pieces of hardware in existence.

The brief was to create an AI assistant experience that would allow anyone walking through the facility — from scientists to executives to curious visitors — to approach the display, have a stylized AI character appear, and ask questions about the quantum machine in natural language.

AI character concepts placeholder
Interaction flow diagram placeholder
The approach

This project pushed into territory that very few UX designers get to work in — the intersection of physical space, AI interaction, and hardware context. The glass display had unique constraints: it had to look beautiful against the quantum machine backdrop, work in variable ambient lighting, and feel inviting rather than intimidating for non-technical visitors.

I collaborated closely with 3D designers and developers to create a visual language for the AI assistant that felt scientifically grounded — not the generic robotic AI aesthetic, but something that genuinely felt like it belonged in a quantum research facility. The assistant's visual design draws on interference patterns, quantum probability fields, and superposition as metaphors.

The interaction model was equally important. How do you design a conversation with an AI about quantum computing for an audience that ranges from Nobel-level physicists to first-time visitors? The answer was layered depth — simple answers on the surface, with the ability to go as deep as the user's curiosity would take them.

Full interface design — glass display placeholder
The outcome

The glass display project is in final design stages and close to implementation. It represents a genuinely new category of interface design — not a screen, not a kiosk, but a spatial AI presence that uses the physical context of the hardware it sits in front of as part of the experience.

This project has been one of the most creatively challenging and rewarding of my career — it required synthesizing visual design, interaction design, AI experience design, and spatial thinking in a way that very few products demand.

QuantumOS

Building a platform from zero — giving researchers and developers the tools to run and monitor quantum machines.

0→1 product systems design developer tools enterprise platform
QuantumOS platform — hero screenshot placeholder
Role
Senior Product Designer
Team
1 other UX designer, engineers, quantum researchers
Timeline
2022 – 2024
Tools
Figma, ReImagine DS, user research sessions
The challenge

Microsoft was selling quantum machines to external research institutions and companies — but what came with the machine? At the time, very little in terms of a unified software experience. Researchers and developers who received a quantum machine needed a way to submit jobs, monitor runs, understand results, and troubleshoot issues. They were doing this with a fragmented collection of command-line tools and spreadsheets.

QuantumOS was the answer: a purpose-built platform for quantum machine operators. I was brought in alongside one other UX designer to take this from zero to a working product, from first sketch to shipped interface.

Early sketches / wireframes placeholder
User flow diagram placeholder
The approach

0→1 design work on a highly technical product for a highly technical audience is both exhilarating and terrifying. We had to establish everything from scratch: the information architecture, the design language, the interaction patterns, the mental models users would bring to the product.

Our primary users were quantum researchers and developers — people with deep domain expertise but varied levels of software experience. Some were command-line native; others preferred GUI tools. The platform had to serve both without condescending to either.

I ran extensive research sessions with potential users at QuNorth and within Microsoft's own quantum team. The biggest insight: users didn't want an abstraction layer on top of the quantum machine, they wanted transparency into what was actually happening. Every design decision was oriented around surfacing ground truth — real machine state, real job status, real results — rather than glossing over complexity.

QuantumOS — job monitoring view placeholder
The outcome

QuantumOS shipped as the primary interface for quantum machine operators at external partner institutions. It consolidated job submission, machine monitoring, and results analysis into a single coherent platform — replacing the fragmented tooling that had existed before.

The project also established a design foundation that could scale as Microsoft's quantum hardware business grows — a design system and component library specifically for quantum computing interfaces, a first of its kind.

Baton Forms

Eliminating the biggest bottleneck in implementation — one form at a time.

0→1 feature B2B SaaS UX research sole designer
Baton Forms — hero screenshot placeholder
Role
Lead Product Designer & UX Researcher (sole designer)
Team
Engineers, customer success, sales
Timeline
8 months
Tools
Figma, Maze, Grain, Jira, Notion
The challenge

Implementation managers at enterprise companies were losing weeks of time to a single, persistent bottleneck: collecting assets from new clients before a project could even kick off. The tools they were using — external forms, email threads, spreadsheets — weren't built for their workflow. The result was constant back-and-forth, delayed timelines, and implementations that started behind schedule before they'd begun.

The feedback from Baton's customers was consistent and direct:

"Getting information from new customers and our sales rep has been a huge bottleneck in our implementations." — Joe M., Built
"We have forms to get info before kick-off, they just don't work — we get a very delayed response. Follow-ups take time." — Osama M., Atrio

The opportunity was clear: build a native forms feature inside Baton so implementation managers could collect, organize, and verify pre-kickoff assets — all within the same product where they were running their projects.

Customer interview insights placeholder
Competitor analysis matrix placeholder
The approach

I led both the research and design for this feature over eight months as the sole designer. Discovery started with deep customer conversations — recurring calls with the implementation managers who would use this every day. I needed to understand not just the pain, but the exact shape of it: what they needed to collect, from whom, at what stage, and what happened when it didn't arrive on time.

A competitor scan followed. Most tools were solving this problem with external form applications bolted onto their products — Google Forms, Typeform, and their equivalents. They all fell short of the specific needs of implementation workflows, which meant the gap was real and the opportunity was ours.

Before any UI work, I worked through the architecture — how Forms would integrate with Baton's existing templated project structure, how a form object would behave in the database, and how it would connect to assets already living in the product. Getting this right early saved weeks downstream.

I brought the full team in early and kept them there. A series of weekly recurring meetings — each with a defined purpose — kept engineers, customer success, and sales reps aligned at every stage. I used Baton itself to document and map the process so the entire team could follow the thread from meeting to milestone.

Testing ran on two tracks simultaneously. Internally, I ran ongoing sessions where customers worked through Figma prototypes on specific tasks — friction points surfaced immediately and fed directly back into the designs. Externally, I used Maze to recruit a broader user pool and gather quantitative and qualitative data that shaped the final calls.

Baton Forms — full interface view placeholder
The handoff

When it was time to hand off to engineering, I wrote a 22,000-word specification document detailing every interaction, state, and edge case in the feature. It was meticulous by design — the goal was to make implementation unambiguous and eliminate the back-and-forth that typically slows a build of this complexity. It worked: deployment time was cut almost in half.

The outcome

Baton Forms launched to immediate, enthusiastic adoption. The impact was concrete: a 30% reduction in development time, a 60% reduction in handoff time, and three new customer deals closed where Forms was cited as the deciding differentiator from competing products.

"This feature is a game changer. No other product in the space has something this seamless and easy to use." — Ben, Ramsoft
"This cuts our pre-implementation time in half. Forms has changed the way we do implementations." — Joe, Built

The process standards built around this project also stuck — the review and documentation approach developed during the 8-month build became permanent team practice, contributing to a sustained improvement in how Baton shipped product.

Baton Design System

Building a design system from zero — and keeping it alive through a company acquisition.

design systems 0→1 cross-functional sole designer
Design system component library — hero screenshot placeholder
Role
Lead Product Designer (sole designer)
Team
Engineering team, PM, post-acquisition stakeholders
Timeline
2022 – 2024
Tools
Figma, Storybook collaboration, tokens
The challenge

When I joined Baton as the sole product designer, there was no design system. There were inconsistent UI patterns scattered across the product, different components doing the same job in different ways, and no single source of truth for how the product should look or behave. Every new feature started from scratch.

As the only designer supporting an entire product and engineering team, I couldn't afford to rebuild the wheel on every project. I needed a system — and I needed to build it while simultaneously shipping product work, with no dedicated time or resources to do so.

Component audit — before state placeholder
Token architecture diagram placeholder
The approach

I took an incremental approach: rather than halting product work to build a system from scratch, I built the system in parallel — codifying components as I designed them, establishing tokens as I made decisions, writing documentation as I went. Every feature became an opportunity to add to the system rather than diverge from it.

The system was built around three core principles: consistency (every instance of a pattern behaves the same way), flexibility (components adapt gracefully to edge cases without breaking), and developer handoff clarity (every component had enough documentation that engineers could implement without needing to ask questions).

I also worked closely with the engineering team to ensure the design system had a code counterpart — not just a Figma library, but shared tokens and naming conventions that bridged design and implementation. This reduced the gap between design intent and shipped product significantly.

Design system — component overview placeholder
Surviving an acquisition

In July 2024, Baton was acquired. I was one of the few people retained, and part of my role in the transition was stewarding the design system through significant product changes. New stakeholders, new requirements, a new codebase to reconcile with — the design system had to evolve while maintaining continuity for existing users.

This phase tested every aspect of the system's architecture. The components that had been built with flexibility held up well. The areas where we had cut corners became painfully obvious. It was one of the most valuable lessons I've had in the long-term cost of design debt.

The outcome

Over two years, the Baton design system grew to cover the full product surface — hundreds of components, a complete token set, interaction guidelines, and accessibility documentation. Design-to-development cycles shortened significantly. New features could be designed and handed off in days rather than weeks.

More than the artifacts, the system created a shared design culture at Baton — a common language between design and engineering that made every conversation about the product more efficient and every shipped feature more consistent.
